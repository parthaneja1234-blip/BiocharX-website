What Is Biochar

Carbon-rich material
from biomass pyrolysis

Biochar is produced by heating agricultural biomass in low-oxygen conditions — a process called pyrolysis. The result is a porous, stable, carbon-rich material that transforms crop waste into a powerful tool for soil health, water retention, and long-term carbon capture.

Soil Fertility

Enhances microbial activity and nutrient retention in degraded soils.

Carbon Lock-in

Sequesters carbon stably for hundreds to thousands of years.

Water Retention

Porous structure holds moisture, cutting irrigation needs by up to 30%.

Crop Yield

Field trials show 10–30% yield increase with consistent application.

Production Technology

The Kon-Tiki Kiln:
Scalable pyrolysis

01

Biomass Collection

Agricultural residue — rice husks, wheat straw, wood chips, sugarcane bagasse — is collected, dried, and prepared. Punjab's abundant crop waste becomes the raw input.

02

Kon-Tiki Kiln Loading

Biomass is loaded into the cone-shaped Kon-Tiki kiln. The conical design creates a natural flame curtain that maintains low-oxygen conditions for pyrolysis without a sealed reactor.

03

Pyrolysis at 400–700°C

Biomass is heated in the absence of oxygen. Organic molecules break down — volatile gases escape as syngas while stable carbon chains remain as the biochar matrix.

04

Quenching & Cooling

Water is added to quench combustion and cool the biochar. This stops further oxidation and locks in the highly porous micropore structure critical for soil activity.

05

Sieving, Testing & Packaging

Biochar is sieved to particle size, tested for pH, carbon content, and surface area, then packaged for direct application or further processing into activated or functional variants.

Kon-Tiki Kiln · Open-Source Design

Open-Source Pyrolysis

The Kon-Tiki design democratizes biochar production. Any farmer can build and operate one with minimal investment, producing high-quality biochar from local agricultural waste.

For Farmers

Simple steps to
transform your soil

📏

Recommended Dosage

Apply 2–5 tonnes per hectare for optimal results. For severely degraded soils, up to 10 t/ha in the first year may be beneficial based on soil test results.

🔄

Mixing Method

Incorporate into the top 15–20cm before sowing. Blend with compost at 1:3 ratio (biochar:compost) for best microbial activation and faster soil response.

🌾

Crop Applications

Effective for wheat, rice, vegetables, and fruit crops. Best applied during land preparation before kharif or rabi season for maximum benefit.

💧

Reduce Irrigation

After biochar application, reduce irrigation frequency by up to 30%. The porous carbon structure retains moisture between waterings, reducing runoff.

🧪

Soil Testing

We recommend a baseline soil test before first application. Our team helps interpret results and customize the biochar blend for your specific soil conditions.

📞

Expert Consultation

Free agronomy consultation for farmers in Punjab and surrounding districts. Contact us for a farm visit and a personalized soil health recommendation.

Sustainability Impact

Climate action through
carbon sequestration

01

Carbon Sequestration

When biochar enters the soil, the carbon locked within it remains stable for hundreds to thousands of years — directly reducing atmospheric CO₂. Each tonne of biochar applied sequesters approximately 2.2 tonnes of CO₂-equivalent.

02

Greenhouse Gas Reduction

Biochar application reduces methane and nitrous oxide emissions from soil by up to 40%, while simultaneously reducing the need for synthetic nitrogen fertilizers that produce GHG through manufacture and application.

03

Stubble Burning Solution

Punjab generates over 20 million tonnes of crop stubble annually. Instead of burning it — causing severe respiratory health crises and air pollution — biochar production converts this waste into a long-term climate asset.

04

Carbon Credits (Coming 2025–26)

We are developing systems to help farmers using BiocharX products participate in voluntary carbon credit markets. Verified carbon removals can generate direct income, making sustainable farming financially rewarding.
